The length of the wall is up to 4 kilometers, Sighnaghi fortress occupies forty hectares. Behind the fortress wall is the Kremkhela area of Moscow, which has an area of 40 hectares. It had 23 towers and 6 arched gates.

All have their own names and according to the villages: Magharos Gate and its two towers, Royal or Jugaan Tower, Kakheti Tower 1 and Kakheti Tower 2, Shield Tower and Shield Gate. "Naringkale" tower, Ziar-Pkhoveli tower, tower near Vakir gate, Vakir gate, Kardenakh tower, Bakurtsikhi tower, Mashnaar gate, its guard Oribisk, Boshki tower, Sakobo tower, Sakobo tower,Machkhaani Tower, Bodbi Tower.

During the enemy's invasion, the entire population of the villages that were building the fortress-wall took refuge here with their livelihood. They were in charge of guarding the towers and walls. The walls of the castle are two-tiered, the thickness of the wall of the lower tier is 1.5 meters, the thickness of the upper tier is 70-80 cm. The difference in the thickness of these walls creates a battle path from the inside, the width of which was sufficient for the movement of fighters. Stone steps were arranged on the sides of the towers and several other places in the fort to climb the path. Most of the towers had a two-story battle room. A church named after St. Stephen is built into the fortress wall.

The Sighnaghi Museum was founded in 1947 as the Museum of Local Lore of Kyzik. Its exposition was opened on December 31, 1950. The total number of exhibits was 700 and the departments of history, social construction and natural science were presented.

To better understand the history and culture of the city, visit the Sighnaghi Museum. There is a permanent exposition of the ethnographic collection, which includes about 5,000 artifacts, as well as archaeological treasures of the Middle Ages, works of famous Georgian artists - Niko Firosmani and Lado Gudiashvili, and others.

Church of St. George of Sighnaghi is located in the city of Sighnaghi in Kakheti. It dates back to the 19th century. The church is a hall-type building, although its outer masses are divided in such a way that it gives the impression of a three-nave church.
